AI in Manufacturing

Why Manufacturing Needs an AI-First Operating Model, Not Just AI Pilots

Most manufacturers run AI as a side project bolted onto legacy operations. The plants that win the next decade will design decisions, staffing, and data flows around AI from the start.

That shift means treating AI as core operating infrastructure rather than a lab experiment: predictive maintenance feeding directly into planning systems, quality models wired into line-stop decisions, and a data foundation clean enough to trust at scale. It also means redesigning roles so operators and engineers work alongside AI outputs daily, not reviewing them as an afterthought once a quarter.

Digital Transformation

Digital Transformation Is a Governance Problem Before It's a Technology One

Technology selection is rarely what derails a transformation program. Unclear decision rights, competing executive sponsors, and absent change governance are what stall it.

A governance layer — a steering committee with real authority, a single backlog with transparent prioritization, and a defined escalation path — turns transformation from a series of disconnected projects into a coherent enterprise program. Get that structure right first, and the technology decisions that follow become dramatically easier to make and to defend.

Cybersecurity

Building a Cyber-Resilient Enterprise: Why Compliance Alone Isn't Resilience

Passing an audit proves a control existed on the day someone checked. Resilience is what happens on the day a control fails — and someone is still watching.

Resilience comes from layering detection, response rehearsal, and recovery testing on top of a compliant baseline: tabletop exercises run with business leaders in the room, backup restores tested on a real schedule rather than assumed to work, and a security culture where employees report anomalies instead of hiding them. Compliance frameworks set the floor; resilience is the discipline of operating above it.

Technology Leadership

From Keeping the Lights On to Setting the Agenda: The Modern CIO Mandate

The CIO role has quietly split in two: one half still defends uptime and budget, the other is expected to shape where the business competes next.

Making that second half real requires the CIO to show up with a point of view on growth, cost, and risk — not just a status report on projects. That means fluency in the language of the P&L, a seat earned by consistently linking technology investment to business outcomes, and the confidence to say no to initiatives that look modern but don't move the business forward.
